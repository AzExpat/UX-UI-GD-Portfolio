UX/UI DESIGN CASE STUDY

Arizona Department of Veterans' Services Responsive Website Redesign

Outdated agency website gets a much needed UX/UI makeover

ADVS Responsive Website Redesign

UX/UI DESIGN

Project Overview

The Arizona Department of Veterans' Services is a state agency that provides critical, state-wide coordination and technical assitance to services and organizations serving US Veterans. The current website is antiquated and confusing. Although it is responsive, it has the look and feel of a much older site, navigation is not intuitive, and information is hard to find.

The Solution:

A complete website reorganization and rebuild. Special attention was paid to ease of navigation and creating visual continuity.

My Role:

UX and UI Designer, Individual Project

Timeline:

4 Weeks

RESEARCH
Usability Testing

During the research phase I conducted 5 user interviews to gain a better understanding of how easy and intuitive the site was navigation was. Users were also given the specific task of finding the “Tuition Waiver Form” page and eligiblity requirements.

"The website it cluttered, if it's not in the (main) menu, I won't look for it any more"
- Devin

"I guess it's ok, IF you already know where to look".
- Amie

Proto Persona

Based on current US veteran demographics provided by the Veterans Administration (https://www.va.gov/vetdata/veteran_population.asp), I created a Proto Persona to help focus on a typical user and keep his/her needs and limitations in the forefront of the design process.

Problem Statement:

How might we redesign the ADVS website to make it easier and faster for Arizona veterans to find information and services available to them?

With almost 70 pages, and countless external links the website seemed to be growing in many different directions at once.
Information Architecture

An updated site map reduced the main navigation links from 7 to 5 and moved the staff login to a more prominent position in the universal header in the top right corner.

A universal footer was created to house common links such as “Contact Us", “Search”, social media links and legal disclaimers.

A main priority based on user testing and my own observation was to make searching the site easier by moving the search function to a far more user friendly location.

IDEATION
Wireframing

After sketching out a few rough ideas, I created basic wireframes in Figma. These wireframes were used to prototype my designs giving me a much clearer picture of how the pages and functions would interact.

Along with moving the Search function to a more prominent postion, I created quick links to the sites most visited pages, aleviating the need to follow menu links or even type in a search topic.

Because there are so many areas of assistance and information available, if the user is unable to quickly locate his or her area of inerest a second search bar eliminates the need to scroll up to the top menu bar if topic is not immediately found.

UI Style Guide
Bumps in the Road

Sourced online, I only found poor quality, mismatched, freeby icons (left). The solution was to create my own (right).

Gold was the original color choice for the "Most Accessed" buttons. It didn't pass accessibility testing however, and needed to be replaced. A darker gold looked muddy and red buttons, later tested, were deemed "Too jarring", by some testers.

TESTING
A/B Testing and User Feedback

After clickable prototypes were built in Figma, I conducted and tested my high fidelity design with 5 volunteers. The goal of the testing was to assess whether users found the navigation easier and more intuitive and whether they could find the information they wanted with less frustration.

The buttons on the left were, "Just too big" according to testers. By resizing them they now fit above the fold.

Additional User Testing Findings:

  • Mobile: Clean up the typography and polish the information hierarchy on the Tuition Waiver page.
  • Mobile: The black hamburger menu was hard to see against the blue background.
  • "There's a lot of blue".

While adding visual interest, the red accordion dropdown menu was deemed, "Too jarring" by users and was scrapped.

THE SOLUTION
Iterated Clickable Prototypes

Tempted to make my subheading read "Final Clickable Prototypes", I'm aware that a design is never really "done", and there will always be something I can revisit. I guess I can say this is my Final Clickable Prototype...for now!

CONCLUSION

Every project presents different challenges and learning opportunities, and this one was no different. One of the more startling things I learned was just how extensive the original site was. It appeared to have been added to and tweaked over the years, rather than reorganized and redesigned. At the onset I thought about how satisfying it would be to really redesign it, by the end of the project however, I knew just how monumental the task would be. I found that Information Architecture is far more interesting that I ever would have guessed, and that even the best thought out color scheme can be problematic.
